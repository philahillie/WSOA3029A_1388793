A CLOSE READING AND REFLECTION ON KNAFLIC’S CHAPTER 6

In the sixth chapter of "Storytelling with Data” by Knaflic, he discusses how data and their representation in graphs can be represented. While graphs serve as a simple representation of data that should be able to be interpreted many people, adding more tools that make the interpretation process easier can be done in a way that is informative and avoids looking cluttered. The graph in model visual #1 makes use of x-axis and y-axis labels and numbers, a heading for the graph, a polyline that changes in line weight and style (stroke to dashed line) and descriptions above the polyline. All of this information is displayed using different line weights and colours so that the graph appears balanced while still being informative. Knaflic explains that using lighter colours for information that provides a broader context can ensure that the graph is not visually overwhelming. He also says that data that is plotted as a single line (or polyline) should have distinguishing aspects and I agree with this sentiment because adding circles at the points in the line graph make the numerical values that the graph is meant to represent easier to read.

Visual hierarchy is an important component because it helps draw the reader's eyes to the information they need to understand in the graph. Words I the form of clear titles, labelling and annotations also make graphs more accessible as they leave less to the imagination and this results in it taking less time to interpret and understand the information being presented.

In Model Visual #3, the graph makes use of stacked bars that have been divided into portions that represent a full 100%. The title of the graph states that it is looking at “Goal attainment over time” but what stands out is the information pertaining to missed goals that has been highlighted in a deep red whilst the information about the goals that have been met or exceeded is portrayed in varying shades of grey. The annotation at the top right also states that more than a third of the projects are missing goals and this makes me wonder if the red is depicting the absence of goals or the fact that the goals have not been achieved. This is where establishing what one wants to communicate, keeping that message consistent and then applying the visual hierarchy to the graph becomes important because what is emphasized does not necessarily reflect what the title states the graph is trying to highlight. In this chapter, I think them main point Knaflic is trying to push forward is the idea of telling a story, providing context and keeping the narrative consistent.
