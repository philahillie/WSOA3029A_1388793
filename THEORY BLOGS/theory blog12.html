ANALYSIS OF DATA DRIVEN ART

The data-driven art that have chosen to analyse is a campaign that looked at translating endangered animal species and the number of animals left into a visual. This project was first developed by Yoshiyuki Mikami and the World Wildlife Fund in 2008. The idea was to create a series of images in which a picture of an animal could only be made using the number of pixels that matched the number of animals of that species that were still alive in the wild (i.e., an image of a Bengal Tiger would be 2500 pixels as only 2500 tigers remained as of 2008). This campaign inspired Joshua Smith, a programmer who took the idea further and developed a Python script (Python is a coding language) that generates images with correct number of pixels. The script generated these images based on the endangered species list from Animal Planet. Although the images were not completely accurate due to the fact that the number of pixels had to adjust themselves to a square image and therefore be multiples of an identical number of pixels (7 x 7 or 8 x 8), the point of the exercise was for viewers to see that someone images were unrecognisable and hard to identify due to the number of animals within that species being low thus the pixilation of the image was bad. The Asian elephant image comprised of between forty thousand and fifty thousand pixels which made them recognisable while animal species such as the black footed ferret (the image was 300 pixels) and the Javan Rhino (the image was 60 pixels) were unidentifiable in comparison, a portrait of a human would need 7.53 billion pixels or 7.53 gigapixels in the image to be accurately portrayed.

When thinking about the endangered species, I understand that the numbers are depleting at a rapid rate and that something needs to be done to ensure that species do not go extinct. However, this project was interesting to me because it allows viewers to see how close these animals are to being eradicated off the face of the earth. The use of images that are so pixelated that they have become unrecognisable appeals to the emotions of loss and memory, insinuating that these animals will become a distant memory. Artistic strategies help explore topics that cannot be put or fully comprehended into words and I feel that project begins to put the topic of endangered animals into a perspective that is easier to understand. When one looks at a pixelated image, they associate it with poor image quality and when I see the pixelated images of the endangered animals, I immediately think of poor quality and the what implications of losing more animal species will have on our environment. This campaign invokes emotions of shock, sadness and sympathy towards the animals whose numbers are dramatically low.
