ETHICS IN DATA-DRIVEN STORYTELLING

I understand the practice of ethical data-driven storytelling as the act of presenting data or information that is as true as possible, transparent and does not mislead end-users when they interpret the information. Stephen Ward describes ethics as "the study and practice of what constitutes the best regulation of human conduct, individually and socially". Nicholas Diakopoulos adds that ethics is about "making sound editorial decisions along each step of the data-driven storytelling process" (Diakopoulos 2018). Diakopoulos makes mention of two principles that pertain to ethical storytelling. The first principle discusses how the relevancy of data stories and details how "opportunities for (mis)guiding end-users' interpretation of data can arise during every phase of production" (Diakopoulos, 2018). These phases of production are the acquisition, quantification, normalisation, aggregation, filtering, visually encoded, annotated and interactive design of data.

The second principle discusses "the ways in which insights from data are found and interpreted as stories" (Diakopoulos, 2018). It is important for the storyteller to trace and disclose their rationale and process for arriving at a preferred interpretation. This principle relates to the acquisition and data transformation steps. The third principle advises that "the individual people who the data represents are to be treated with humanity and respect and not be seen only as a means for telling a story" (Diakopoulos, 2018). It connects to the interactivity component of data-driven storytelling and is a way for designers to engage with the community. There are several steps in the process of data-driven storytelling that are crucial to communicating information in an ethical manner that can be understood and interpreted factually.

Within the acquisition step of data, provenance aims to "provide a baseline of transparency" (Diakopoulos, 2018) and addresses the legal or illegal acquiring of the data. It also looks at who produced the data, what the intent of getting the data was and whether the data is complete, timely and accurate. Quantification deals with how measurements of the data are defined. Withing the data transformation process, normalisation gives the end-user more freedom to make interpretation decisions on their terms. I understand this to be the process of finding a mean or average in which the data can be presented accurately but still factor in changes or the possibility of differing interpretations. Aggregated data is "data that is derived through mathematical operations or combinations" (Diakopoulos, 2018). It is "a consistent form of aggregation that is applied to any data that are to be visually compared" (Diakopoulos, 2018). Steps such as filtering, conveying and connecting insights, being mindful of implied relationships and giving data context and annotations making it interactive help with the ethical communication of data-driven storytelling and, when does right, minimise opportunities more misleading end-users.

Reading this chapter made me think about the way in which I have communicated the ratings and television series I have discussed in my blogs. My website deals with subjective metrics and opinions on what makes a film or series good and thus I think it is important that I site the sources I am getting my data from so that end-users are not under the impression that the ratings I am presenting are what has been provided by the whole population but rather by companies or organisations that could potentially have their own biases. This is why I was inclined to at least show male and female perspective so people could see what the effect of gender may have on the ratings.
