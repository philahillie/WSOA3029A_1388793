HOW ART CAN CONTRIBUTE TO ETHICAL REFLECTION ON RISKY TECHNOLOGIES

When something is ethical, it can be described as "relating to moral principles" (Oxford Languages). In her paper, Sabine Roeser describes risky technologies as technologies that explore cloning and human enhancement, the hybridity of humans and animals and hyperconnectivity. Roeser discusses how art has performed as a tool that bridges the gap of understanding and allows people to see the possibilities of risky technologies that are commonly not trusted by people. The emergence of techno art – art that reflects on and engages with risky technologies – has allowed for new and more open-minded views and interpretations of risky technologies to be engaged with.

Historical examples of techno art would be the works of Leonardo da Vinci who explored scientific developments within art. Many other artists took a "critical stance" and movements such as Romanticism explored ideal world in a pre-industrialised world (Roeser). Examples of techno art such as Frankenstein by Mary Shelley have shaped people's suspicions towards technologies such as human enhancement while others such as science fiction films have embraced technology and have developed dystopian environments in which the possibilities of risky technologies can be explored and communicated in a way that can appeal to audiences. This has been done through the use of aesthetics, emotion and the communication of ideas in a imaginative, provocative but playful way (Roeser).

Techno art engages with new technologies and presents opportunities for new conversations around technological developments to be had. Stelarc, a performance artist, developed a project that was an intersection of biology and art and explored the ways in which we connect to each other in an age of hyperconnectivity. In his project, he attached a third ear to this arm through the use of surgery and using his own stem cells. He proposed that this was part of an extended Bluetooth that could serve as a way in which people could connect to each other more intimately. The "Ear On Arm" would become and "internet organ for the body" (Roeser) which would eventually be developed into a wireless connection that could be implanted into the ear. This exploration of stem cell research goes beyond the clinical approach that is presented by scientist and rather explores it in a somewhat practical conceptual format.

Art invokes emotions and thus encourages critical thinking and reflection from the emotional experience of engaging with the work, Art can help audiences replace the barrier of suspicion with curiosity and this can lead to people being more open minded (Roeser). Generally, I have not had a huge interest in stem cell research or human enhancement but seeing an ear on someone's arm definitely sparked my interested and made me wonder why and how that was done. This is how people can get more knowledge and feel a part of the larger conversation surrounding risky technologies.
