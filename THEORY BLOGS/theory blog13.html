CRITICAL REFLECTION ON MY DATA-DRIVEN ARTWORK

In my previous blog post, I analysed a data-driven artwork designed by Yoshiyuki Mikami and further explored by Joshua Smith in which images of endangered animals were compiled with the same number of pixels as there are those animals in real life. I found this campaign powerful because it gave a simple visual representation of the dwindling number of animals left within the respective endangered animal species. The images that depicted animals that were on the verge of extinction as heavily pixelated and barely recognisable and thus played on the idea of the animals becoming a distant memory or part of our past history due to their impending extinction. As someone who responds to visuals quicker than text, I appreciated being able to interpret the message of this campaign quickly.

According to Nicholas Diakopoulos, data-driven storytelling should "seek truth and report it as fully as possible", "be transparent" and "engage the community" (Diakopoulos, 2018). An element of data-driven storytelling that I believe was used well was the idea of quantification which looks at how measurements are defined. Quantification considers whether what is measured in the data allows for a given story to be faithful to reality (Diakopoulos, 2018) and I believe that pixilation of the images of the animals is a smart way to quantify the existing umber of animals within a species are left and the reality that it reveals does not mislead end-users. It highlights how dire the circumstance of animals on the endangered species list is. The artists further provided context for the data by mentioning that the endangered animal species list and its numbers were provided by National Geographic. This gives the data credibility (as it is an organisation considered with providing information about animals). While the images cannot be 100% accurate with regard to relaying the exact number of animals left to the number of pixels in the image, the message is still conveyed and gives the end-users insight into the campaign (Diakopoulos). The additional annotation minimises the possibility of anything detracting from the message of the artwork.

When I think of my data-driven artworks, I also think of the space that they exist in, my website. This has informed the style guide of my website as I want the data-driven storytelling to blend well into the narrative of my site. I view my site as the context that will aid in minimising the end-user from being distracted.
